Aim

The goal of this blog is to understand the asymptotic behavior of the stringy05 index, defined as

\[ \text{stringy05} = \frac{{diameter}(T_n)}{{length}(T_n)}. \]

Here \(T_n\) is the Euclidean minimum spanning tree (MST) built on \(n\) random bivariate observations.

The denominator is the total Euclidean length of the MST,

\[ L_n = {length}(T_n) = \sum_{e\in T_n}|e|. \]

The numerator is the weighted graph diameter of the MST,

\[ D_n = {diameter}(T_n) = \max_{u,v\in T_n} d_{T_n}(u,v), \]

where (d_{T_n}(u,v)) is the length of the unique path in the tree from (u) to (v), using Euclidean edge lengths as weights.

Thus,

The main question is whether

or whether

[ R_n > 0. ]

Empirically, previous simulations suggested that the ratio does not rapidly decay toward zero. Around sample sizes near (n=900), the estimated 95th percentile of the noise distribution appeared to stabilize near (0.2). This motivates a more detailed investigation of the numerator and denominator separately.

Denominator: theoretical growth rate of total MST length

The denominator (L_n) is the part of the problem for which we do have strong theoretical guidance.

Steele studies Euclidean minimal spanning trees with power-weighted edges. In his notation, for points (x_1,,x_n), the MST functional is

[ M(x_1,,x_n) = T {eT}(|e|), ]

where the minimum is over spanning trees (T), and () is an edge-weighting function [Steele, 1988].

So Steele’s (M_n) is exactly the denominator of the stringy05 index.

However, one technical caveat is important. Steele’s theorem is stated under compact-support assumptions. A bivariate Gaussian distribution has unbounded support. Therefore, for Gaussian samples, Steele’s result should be used as a theoretical benchmark unless an additional tail-truncation or extension argument is supplied.

Numerator: what is known about the MST diameter?

The numerator is

[ D_n = {u,v}d{T_n}(u,v). ]

This is the weighted longest shortest path in the MST. It is a global path functional: it depends on how many edges lie along the longest path and how their lengths accumulate.

This is different from the total MST length (L_n), which is a sum over all edges. It is also different from the longest single edge of the MST.

At this point, the exact theoretical growth rate of

[ D_n ]

is not given by the Steele MST length theorem.

Penrose’s longest-edge theorem and its limitation

Penrose studies the longest edge of the random Euclidean MST. Let

[ E_n = _{eT_n}|e|. ]

For uniform random points in the unit square, Penrose shows that the longest MST edge has the same asymptotic extreme-value behavior as the longest nearest-neighbor edge. In dimension (2), the result implies that

[ nE_n^2-n ]

converges in distribution to a double-exponential limiting law [Penrose, 1997].

He also proves that, with probability tending to one, every sufficiently long MST edge is also a nearest-neighbor edge and has one endpoint at a leaf of the MST. This is important because it suggests that the very longest MST edges are typically peripheral leaf edges, not necessarily edges that form the interior of the diameter path.

The longest edge scale is therefore approximately

[ E_n . ]

However, this is not the same as the stringy05 numerator.

The stringy05 numerator is

[ D_n = {u,v} d{T_n}(u,v), ]

which is the length of an entire path. This path may contain many MST edges. Therefore, Penrose’s theorem does not give the growth rate of (D_n). It only gives the scale of the largest individual edge.

A deterministic sandwich for the diameter

Even though Penrose’s longest-edge theorem does not solve the diameter problem, it provides a weak lower bound.

[ D_n = {a,b} d{T_n}(a,b) d_{T_n}(u,v) = |e| = E_n. ]

What the theory currently tells us

The theoretical results give a clear picture for the denominator:

[ L_n=(n). ]

[ D_n O(n). ]

The lower bound comes from the longest MST edge, while the upper bound comes from the total MST length. But this interval is too wide to determine the true growth rate of (D_n).

Previous simulation evidence

Previously, I ran simulations to estimate the 95th percentile of the noise distribution of the stringy05 index. For each sample size, I used 100,000 repetitions to estimate the 95th percentile.

The fitted model for stringy05 did not show clear convergence toward zero. Instead, for sample sizes around (n=900), the estimated 95th percentile appeared to stabilize around (0.2).

This suggests that the numerator and denominator may be growing on the same order, but the previous simulation studied the ratio directly. To understand the mechanism better, the next step is to separate the numerator and denominator.

For each (n), I will compute Monte Carlo summaries of

The main plots will be:

  1. A log-log plot of (L_n) versus (n).
  2. A log-log plot of (D_n) versus (n).

Summary

The theoretical denominator is well understood:

[ L_n=(n) ]

for two-dimensional Euclidean MSTs under Steele’s framework.

The numerator is not yet theoretically pinned down. Penrose’s longest-edge theorem gives only a weak lower bound because the longest edge is not the same as the weighted MST diameter.

The deterministic inequality

[ E_nD_nL_n ]

places the numerator between the longest-edge scale and the total-length scale. But this range is too wide to determine whether

[ R_n= ]

converges to zero or to a positive constant.

The empirical evidence so far suggests that the stringy05 index stabilizes near (0.2). The next step is to test this more directly by estimating the growth rates of (D_n) and (L_n) separately.
